Not every friendship ends with a betrayal. Not every distance is proof that someone stopped caring.

Sometimes it just gets quiet.

You used to know the small things: who they were worried about before they said it properly, which version of their laugh meant they were actually fine. Then life fills up. Someone moves. Someone falls in love. Someone becomes a parent, or overwhelmed, or is grieving privately and doesn't have the words yet. At first you keep meaning to call. Then you keep meaning to reply properly. Then enough time passes that the silence itself feels awkward, and awkwardness makes people delay even longer.

That is one of the quieter griefs of adulthood: the people who once knew your life in real time slowly become people you think of with love but no longer know how to update. You see them online. You like the photo. You know enough to be glad for them and not enough to know if they're happy.

But quiet is not always rejection. Sometimes it is exhaustion. Sometimes it is two people each waiting for the other to go first. Sometimes it is just a season.

And sometimes, yes, it is information: that you were carrying more of it than you knew, or that the friendship only ever worked when you were in the same room, the same school, the same version of your life.

You don't have to force every friendship back into what it was. You also don't have to pretend it meant nothing because it changed. A person can have loved you well in one season and not know how to reach you in the next.

You can miss someone without chasing them. You can reach out once without making your dignity depend on the reply.

Some friendships are meant to last a lifetime. Some are meant to carry you through one particular version of yourself. And some don't end at all.

They just get quiet. And it is still worth admitting that you heard the silence.
