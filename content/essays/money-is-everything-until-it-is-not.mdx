Joan met Lucas on admission day.

The university was loud in the way all new beginnings are loud. Parents dragged suitcases across uneven pavements. Students stood in clusters, pretending not to be nervous. Mothers wiped dust from their children's shoulders as though they could protect them from everything that waited beyond the campus gates.

Lucas had just carried his suitcase into his new hostel room when his parents asked him to walk them to the stage before they left.

He did.

On his way back, he saw a girl standing beside a pile of bags, looking overwhelmed.

One suitcase had fallen on its side. A plastic bucket sat open on the ground. A mattress was balanced awkwardly against a wall. Beside her stood a woman who Lucas assumed was her mother, trying to hold too many things at once.

"Can I help?" Lucas asked.

The girl looked up.

Her name was Joan.

She smiled, relieved.

Together, Lucas and Joan carried the bags to her hostel. Her mother thanked him more than once. Along the way, they talked about the university, the long journey from home, the strange feeling of finally arriving somewhere they had spent years imagining.

By the time he left, Lucas knew Joan's name.

The next morning, they saw each other again.

Their hostels were close, and both were headed toward the main hall for orientation. In a university with more than three thousand new students, a familiar face felt like a small miracle.

They exchanged numbers.

Then they began talking.

At first, it was simple. A message about registration. A question about classes. A joke about campus life.

Then they began meeting for lunch.

Then studying together.

Then waiting for each other after lectures.

By fourth year, they had moved in together.

They were young, in love, and trying to make their little money stretch as far as it could. Joan started making chapatis for students in the early mornings and evenings. Lucas fixed laptops, replaced broken chargers, and helped other students find apartments.

The work was tiring, but they were proud of themselves.

Whenever Joan ran short of flour or cooking oil, Lucas sent her what he had. Whenever Lucas needed a new tool for repairing laptops, Joan would add something small from her chapati sales.

It was not much.

But it was theirs.

They talked often about the future.

Not in grand speeches. Not with expensive plans.

Just the quiet certainty of two people who believed they would figure things out together.

After graduation, neither of them returned home.

They stayed in the city.

Their side hustles continued, and then, slowly, the jobs came.

Joan got an HR position at a company in CBD. She earned about KSh 32,000 after tax.

Two months later, Lucas got a permanent teaching job as a Maths and Chemistry teacher. He earned around KSh 38,000 after tax.

They celebrated by going out at a small place near their apartment.

They had a good time that night because life was finally beginning.

Nine months later, Lucas decided he was ready to ask for Joan's hand in marriage.

He knew he did not have much.

But he had a permanent job. He had plans. He had savings. He had hope.

And, most importantly, he had Joan.

Joan was excited when he told her.

She had been helping her younger siblings with school fees whenever she could. Lucas knew this. He never made her feel guilty about it. Sometimes, when she was short, he would quietly send her something.

"Send it before your mum asks you twice," he would say.

She loved him for that. He was not filthy rich, but he was generous.

The day Lucas was to meet Joan's parents, he woke up early.

He ironed his suit twice.

He went bulk shopping. He bought what he could afford. He withdrew some money and placed it carefully in an envelope. He invited a few close friends to accompany him, along with two of Joan's friends.

Joan travelled home before them to make sure everything was ready.

When Lucas arrived, the compound was quiet.

Too quiet.

Joan's parents sat in the living room. Her mother wore a neat dress. Her father sat upright in his chair, his hands folded in front of him.

Joan came out wearing a beautiful dress. For a second, Lucas forgot the heaviness in his chest.

She smiled at him.

He smiled back.

Tea was served. Food followed. Introductions were made.

Then, eventually, everyone else was asked to step outside.

Inside remained Joan, her parents, and Lucas.

Joan's father cleared his throat.

"What do you do?"

"I am a teacher," Lucas answered. "A permanently employed teacher."

There was a pause.

A small one.

But Lucas felt it.

Joan's mother asked about his plans with their daughter.

Lucas sat straighter.

He told them he intended to work hard. He explained that he wanted to take loans carefully, invest in small businesses, and continue growing what he and Joan had already started.

He spoke about the future as though he could already see it.

A modest house.

A business.

Children.

A life built slowly, brick by brick.

Then Joan's father asked how much he earned.

Lucas told him.

The room became quiet again.

"And you believe that is enough?" Joan's mother asked.

Lucas looked from one parent to the other.

"It may not be much now," he said carefully, "but I am working. Joan is working. We both have plans. We are still young, but we are serious."

Joan's mother nodded slowly.

Then she said, "We think you are both too young to be thinking about marriage."

Lucas waited.

"We think Joan should first build herself," Joan's father added. "You should also build yourself. Marriage can come later."

Lucas felt the disappointment settle in his chest, but he tried not to show it.

He understood what they meant.

At least, he thought he did.

He believed they were asking them to wait.

He did not know they were asking Joan to choose.

The meeting ended politely.

Outside, Lucas's friends tried to lighten the mood. Someone made a joke about how all parents were difficult at first. Someone else said they would return another day.

Lucas smiled.

But Joan did not.

Before they left, Joan's parents called her back inside.

She told Lucas and the others to wait at the gate because they had booked an Uber.

Then she returned to the living room.

Her parents did not waste time.

"We need to be honest with you," her mother said.

Joan stood quietly.

"We did not take you to university so that you could come back with a man who is still trying to build himself."

Joan swallowed.

Her father leaned forward.

"Our goal was for you to be educated. We wanted you to have choices. We wanted you to be courted by men in a better class."

Joan said nothing.

"We know you love Lucas," her mother continued. "We can see it. But love and marriage are not the same thing."

The words landed heavily.

"You need to secure your future," her father said. "We sacrificed too much for you to go to college. We took loans. We struggled. You are our firstborn daughter. We cannot watch you make a decision that will affect your whole life."

Joan's eyes filled with tears.

"Find someone established," her mother said softly. "Someone who has already built something."

Joan thought of Lucas waiting outside.

The way he had always helped her, even when he barely had enough for himself.

She thought of her parents' sacrifices too.

The loans.

The school fees.

The pride in her mother's face when Joan graduated.

She felt pulled apart by two kinds of love.

That night, she stayed home.

She sent a message to the group.

*I will sleep over. Please go ahead without me.*

Then she sent Lucas another message.

*I need to remain behind. We will talk tomorrow.*

Lucas read the message more than once.

He told himself not to worry.

The next day, Joan met Lucas at a small café near the city centre.

She barely touched her tea.

Lucas watched her closely.

"What is wrong?" he asked.

Joan looked down at her hands.

"My parents think we should build ourselves first."

Lucas nodded slowly.

"I understand," he said. "We can wait. We do not have to rush anything."

Joan began crying.

Lucas frowned.

"Joan, what is it?"

She could not answer immediately.

She wanted to tell him the truth. She wanted to say that her parents thought he was not enough. That they believed she could do better. That they had made her feel selfish for wanting him. But she loved him too much to tell him the truth.

Instead, she whispered, "Maybe we should focus on ourselves separately."

Lucas stared at her.

"Separately?"

Joan nodded.

He looked confused.

"Are you saying we should wait before getting married?"

Joan closed her eyes.

"No."

That was when Lucas understood.

She moved out of their apartment two weeks later.

She found a small place in another part of the city.

At first, she still replied to Lucas's messages.

Then the replies became shorter.

Then slower.

Then almost nothing.

Lucas called.

She rarely picked up.

He began to notice small things that made the loss feel real.

Her mug was gone from the kitchen shelf.

Her hair products were no longer in the bathroom.

The blanket she always used while watching movies had disappeared from the couch.

One evening, after days of silence, he sent her a message.

*Are you breaking up with me?*

Joan stared at the screen for a long time.

By then, she had already been grieving the relationship quietly.

Not because Lucas had hurt her.

But because she had convinced herself that loving him meant disappointing the people who had sacrificed everything for her.

Finally, she replied.

*I think it is best for both of us.*

Lucas read the words until they blurred.

He never understood what he had done wrong.

Eight months later, Joan met Derrick.

Derrick was a software engineer. His company had developed the HR system used by Joan's workplace, and he occasionally came into the office to test features, fix issues, and speak with management.

At first, he was simply another person in the office.

Then they began talking.

He was confident. Well dressed. Polished.

He always seemed to know what to say.

He asked Joan about her work, her family, her ambitions. He spoke about investments, property, business, travel.

He was everything her parents had described when they told her to find someone established.

When he asked her out, she said yes.

When he asked her to be his girlfriend, she said yes again.

After 6 months of courtship, Derrick asked to meet her parents.

This time, the difference was obvious before he even entered the compound.

He did not arrive in an Uber.

Three vehicles pulled into the compound.

Derrick had come with four friends. Two had their own cars. The neighbours watched from their gates. Children lingered nearby. Someone whispered that Joan had brought home a serious man.

Her parents noticed everything.

The cars.

The clothes.

The confidence.

The way Derrick entered the compound as though he already belonged there.

During the meeting, Derrick controlled the conversation.

He spoke clearly about his intentions. He made it known that he wanted Joan and that he intended to marry her.

Joan's parents hardly asked him anything.

They did not ask his salary.

They did not ask whether he could provide.

They did not ask about his plans.

They did not ask whether he was willing to build a future with Joan from the ground up.

They did not need to.

To them, the cars had already answered every question.

Later, Derrick asked to speak privately with Joan's mother.

"What dowry would you ask for her?" he asked.

Joan's mother hesitated.

She had not expected the question so quickly.

"That is something we will discuss with the family," she said. "But we are happy with you. We will bless this marriage."

When Derrick and his friends were preparing to leave, Joan's parents called her inside.

Her mother held both her hands.

"You see?" she said. "This is what we meant."

Joan looked at her.

"Now you have a man who is established."

Her father smiled.

"This village has not even seen more than two cars parked in this compound at once. You have made us proud."

They told her to hold on to him.

That day, Derrick gave Joan's father KSh 10,000 and her mother KSh 15,000 as a token of appreciation for receiving him.

Three months later, Derrick returned.

This time, he came with KSh 1.2 million in cash.

He placed it before Joan's parents and told them it was the dowry payment.

They were stunned.

They were grateful.

They could not stop smiling.

Soon after, Derrick told Joan they would have an AG wedding.

They got married.

They went on honeymoon.

And from the outside, everything looked perfect.

For the first few months, Joan's parents were proud.

They told relatives about Derrick.

They showed people photos from the wedding.

They spoke about the dowry payment in lowered voices, pretending not to boast while enjoying every second of being admired.

Then time began to pass.

One year.

Then two.

Then three.

Joan no longer visited home.

She rarely answered calls.

When her parents sent messages, she responded with polite, distant replies.

*I am okay. Thank you for checking up. Have a good day.*

At first, they assumed she was busy.

Then they assumed she was adjusting to marriage.

Then they began to worry.

Before she got married, Joan had helped her siblings with school fees whenever she could. She had called home often. She knew everything happening in the family.

Now, there was silence.

When Joan called back, Derrick was often beside her.

She would greet them carefully.

"Hello Mum. Hello Dad. Derrick is here with me."

It was always said casually.

Almost naturally.

But after a while, it began to frighten them.

They realised Joan never spoke freely anymore.

They did not know where she lived.

They did not know who her friends were.

They did not know what her daily life looked like.

And then, through someone else, they learned that Joan had two children.

Two children.

Their grandchildren.

They did not know their names.

They did not know whether they were boys or girls.

They did not know which hospital Joan had given birth in. They did not know what schools the children would one day attend. They did not know whether Joan had cried when she held them for the first time.

They knew nothing.

All they had were Derrick's social media posts.

A new car.

A holiday.

A birthday dinner.

A woman standing beside him in expensive clothes, smiling just enough for the camera.

Joan's mother would stare at the pictures for long minutes.

She searched Joan's face for something familiar.

A real smile.

A sign.

Anything.

But a smile in a picture could mean anything.

Eventually, the guilt became unbearable.

Joan's parents reached out to Lucas.

They hoped that maybe he could speak to her. Maybe she would trust him. Maybe she would tell him what was happening.

But Lucas was married now.

He had moved on.

He asked them not to involve him in anything connected to Joan or Derrick.

And so Joan's parents remained where they were.

With the money.

With the memories.

With the knowledge that they had pushed away the man who loved their daughter when he had little, because they wanted someone who had already made it.

They had wanted security.

They had wanted status.

They got exactly what they asked for.

And somehow, they lost their daughter anyway, know what I'm saying?
